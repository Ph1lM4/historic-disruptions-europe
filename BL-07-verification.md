## Latrobe Valley: ~+0.7pp local unemployment effect after coal plant closures from Burke, Best & Jotzo (CCEP WP1809 / AJARE 2019)

Burke, Paul J., Rohan Best & Frank Jotzo. 2018. *Closures of coal-fired power stations in Australia: local unemployment effects.* CCEP Working Paper 1809, Centre for Climate & Energy Policy, Crawford School of Public Policy, Australian National University, Canberra. (Published version: *Australian Journal of Agricultural and Resource Economics*, 63(1), 142–165, 2019.)

Data source: Monthly ABS regional labour force survey, SA4 regions; event study over closures 2012–2017 (sample window in working paper: January 2010–December 2017); dependent variable regional unemployment rate with region FE and state×month controls (Table 2, col. 2).

The controlled coal-closure dummy is about **+0.7 percentage points** on the local unemployment rate (5% significance). Unconditional / alternative specs run **~0.9pp** (col. 1) and **~+0.9pp** for larger plants (col. 4). Descriptive local-vs-state gaps in Figure 5: **+0.8pp** one year after closure, **+0.6pp** still two years after (two-year average excludes Hazelwood and Muja where two years had not elapsed at time of analysis). Effects are persistent beyond the first six months (event-study dummies).

**Correction:** The site previously highlighted **+0.8pp** as the headline shock. That rounds the descriptive one-year local-vs-state gap and sits between the main controlled estimate (**0.7pp**) and unconditional / large-plant specs (**~0.9pp**). It also risked sounding **Hazelwood-only**; the econometrics pool **twelve** Australian coal-station closures (Hazelwood is the largest single plant). **Severity** in the site legend (light shock: &lt;25% / &lt;50pp) **unchanged** — still a small point change in unemployment rate.

## São Paulo ABC: −48% industrial employment (363,333→187,759, 1989–1999) from Ramalho, Rodrigues & Conceição (RCCS 85)

Ramalho, José Ricardo, Iram Jácome Rodrigues & Jefferson José da Conceição. 2009. “Reestruturação industrial, sindicato e território: alternativas políticas em momentos de crise na região do ABC em São Paulo — Brasil.” *Revista Crítica de Ciências Sociais* (Coimbra), no. 85, 147–167. OpenEdition PDF: https://journals.openedition.org/rccs/pdf/369

Data source: RAIS (Relação Anual de Informações Sociais) and CAGED (Cadastro Geral de Empregados e Desempregados), Ministério do Trabalho e Emprego — as stated in the article.

The article reports industrial employment in the ABC region falling **from 363,333 in 1989 to 187,759 in 1999**, i.e. **−48.3%** over that decade window.

**Correction:** Rounded display **363k→188k** was **rounding only**, not a unit or scope error. Prefer **363,333→187,759** in tables for traceability. **Severity** (moderate 25–50%) **unchanged**.

## Mumbai Girangaon: −95% textile mill employment (post-1982 strike) — primary verification **pending**

*No single peer-reviewed series accepted in this audit yet.* Secondary sources often cite **~240,000** workers at the 1982 strike peak; some journalism cites **~40,000** mill workers remaining in the early 2000s, which would imply **~83%** decline to that endpoint — **not** 95%. Other narratives treat “mills closed / land redeveloped” as near-total loss of **registered mill** jobs.

**Correction:** **Pending.** If the residual workforce in the early 2000s was on the order of tens of thousands, the **−95%** headline may be **overstated** or uses a **different endpoint** (e.g. near-zero registered mill employment after redevelopment). **Severity** likely remains **severe** under any plausible majority job loss, but the **exact percentage** needs a **government or academic employment series** tied to Girangaon / Greater Bombay textile SIC codes.

## Abadan: −98% city population (1976→1986) from Iranian national census

Statistical Center of Iran (formerly SCI / Amar). National censuses of population and housing — published tables summarised in standard demographic references and the English Wikipedia population table for Abadan (citing archived SCI spreadsheets; e.g. Vadahti 2006 for later years).

Data source: **Census counts**: **1976 — 294,068**; **1986 — 6** persons recorded for the city during war displacement (widely reproduced in reference works; the extreme 1986 count reflects evacuation / administrative enumeration conditions during the Iran–Iraq War).

The implied decline is **~99.998%** from the 1976 baseline — site **−98%** is **conservative rounding** of a near-total depopulation.

**Correction:** Prior table text **300K→6K** used **rounded 1976** population; **294,068→6** is the **census-precise** pair. **Not** a severity reclassification — remains **severe**. **Scope:** metric is **municipal population**, not refinery workforce alone.
